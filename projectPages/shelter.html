ShelterCase

DSGN 308: Human-Centered Product Design

This class tasked each team to find a problem through user research and participate in the full human-centered design process.

We focused on ensuring that our product would meet the needs of our users. Throughout the course, I practiced and developed observational methods, brainstorming, rapid prototyping, user testing, business models, and the social, marketing, and engineering concerns that impinge upon products.

Problem Finding

Initial Research

To ensure our project was truly human-centered, each team member spent time performing in-person observations, interviews, and sending out surveys to gain insight into the many different problems people similar to our situation encounter, as well as problems that people in other stages of life encounter. After accumulating more than 100 perceived problems, we used three different elimination matrices with weighted criteria to narrow down our problem spaces to our final two options.

Weighing Our Options

One option was improving the takeout experience, as all students needed to take out meals on campus due to COVID, and our other was the option we decided to go with- addressing homelessness. Takeout was a personal project that we would easily have access to users for, while homelessness would present many more barriers with accessing users. However, the challenge that addressing homelessness presented, along with the great possibility for social impact excited us much more than looking at takeout solutions. Even though we knew this problem space would present a large challenge, our passion for the project led us to believe that we would be able to create a prototype that would meet the needs of those experiencing homelessness.

Ideation

Defining the Problem

There are many different problems that those experiencing homelessness face, so we first needed to determine which aspects were important for us to include in our prototype. We determined that people experiencing homelessness struggle to find comfort and safely secure their belongings. We also saw a whitespace in shelter solutions that were highly mobile and promoted independence for the user. This led us to want to create a comfortable shelter solution that allowed users to be mobile and independent, while also offering some kind of secure storage solution.

Using those criteria as guidelines, we started to brainstorm different solutions. The most promising idea was my following sketch, which was a backpack that could be used for storage and expand into a shelter. We thought this idea had great promise and many of those we interviewed used backpacks to transport their belongings and the concept could meet all of the requirements we set.

Initial Concept Validation

To validate this idea, we used the sketch above and asked people what they thought of our concept on the streets. While the feedback for the sentiment was positive in that they believed we were focusing on the right things, everyone we interviewed mentioned the same thing- that there would be a security issue. All interviewees said that they had had their backpacks slashed and belongings stolen. One person stated that he had gotten his backpack slashed while using his backpack as a pillow on the train. Others said that even when they had backpacks with “slash-proof” material, they still had their bags cut and belongings stolen.

Going Back to the Drawing Board

With this new information, we knew it was impractical to continue with the backpack concept. However, we liked a lot of aspects of the original design and thought that the accordion-style expanding motion provided many opportunities. We knew that we needed to have a different base storage method though. The result of a long design brainstorming session resulted in us realizing that a suitcase would be a great solution due to its portability, large storage capacity, ability to be made from more durable materials to prevent theft, and the fact that a couple of interviewees actually used suitcases as their choice method of storage and transportation for their belongings.

Creating ShelterCase

Designing ShelterCase

Once we decided on the suitcase format, we needed to figure out how all aspects of the ideas we had could be incorporated into the design. The main problem was how to expand the suitcase into the bed. We started out with bed slats that would come out from the interior of the suitcase and could stretch out to form a platform. We knew that elevating the bed into a platform was something that would set us apart from sleeping bags and tents as the additional space would protect against weather better. However, bed slats can be hard to manufacture, and so many moving parts meant that it was hard to support and could be unwieldy for users to take out of the box and manipulate. This led us to look at putting the bed slats outside of the suitcase, and finally to our final design of having the platform be a solid trifold base, which was much easier both to manufacture and for users to manipulate. This idea was validated through patent research usign Derwent Innovate.

Making ShelterCase

Once we had this core concept, we turned to Solidworks to figure out the details of the design. Once we had a functional, detailed CAD model we brought renderings to the Evanston Hospitality Center to get feedback from users about our concept. Those at the shelter stated that they much preferred the suitcase design over the backpack idea, and that the locking mechanism along with the ability to transform and be portable were the aspects they thought were most important.

Creating a Physical Prototype

This gave us confidence in our design and allowed us to move forward with creating our physical prototype. While we ran into a couple manufacturing issues, like not accounting for the size of the hinge in how the trifold would operate and lay and how welding would warp the steel legs, we could use the vast majority of our CAD design for exact measurements, directions and placement due to our care and duediligence in the earlier stages of the design process.

Prototype Validation

After creating our prototype, we wanted to validate our concept with users, as we wanted the ShelterCase to be a product that those experiencing homelessness would use. I brought our physical prototype, along with one other group member, to the Evanston Hospitality Center. We were able to show our prototype to both those experiencing homelessness and those who work at the shelter. The physical prototype was slightly awkward, hard to maneuver, and not balanced well. Despite these and many other flaws, we were able to communicate the main aspects of the project and showcase all the desired functionality of our product. The feedback we received was extremely positive with one potential user stating that he could see himself using our prototype now even with all of the current flaws.

Takeaways

Confidence in Leadership

For this project, I was the only senior amongst a group of juniors. My additional year plus the experience that I had and was concurrently getting in my capstone project allowed me a greater amount of insight into the design process. As an outsider, I was initially worried that they would think I was dominating the project and think that because of my additional knowledge that I was better than them. However, when it came to going through the design process I knew from previous experiences some new techniques could be very beneficial. As I introduced these different techniques to the group, I was able to act as a mentor figure to my team members and help guide them, and I gained confidence in my knowledge and leadership abilities.

Importance of Human-Centered Design

Though all Northwestern projects follow the “human-centered design” process, before this project I was simply going through the motions. The ease of the pandemic allowed us to interact safely with users in person and I was able to see the importance this project had to users. Previous projects were always for a client, so I could see the impact it made on them but never for the actual end user. Seeing those experiencing homelessness react to our product truly put into perspective the significance of good design in user’s lives, and how meeting the true needs of the user is the most important goal of all.
